Case File #001 — Status: Open

Where in the World is Satoshi Nakamoto?

On October 31, 2008, a pseudonymous figure published a nine-page paper that would reshape global finance. Then they vanished. The case remains open.

Whitepaper (Primary) → Whitepaper (Archive) →

Satoshi Nakamoto is the pseudonym used by the person or group who created Bitcoin, authored its white paper, and built its original software. After communicating with early developers for roughly two years, Satoshi disappeared in mid-2011, leaving behind approximately one million unmoved bitcoins and one of the greatest unsolved mysteries of the internet age.

Adam Back

British Cryptographer & CEO of Blockstream
Nationality
British
Born
1970
Known For
Hashcash (cited in Bitcoin white paper)
Accused By
NYT / John Carreyrou (2026)
Response
Denies claim
Inventor of Hashcash, the proof-of-work system directly cited in the Bitcoin white paper. Satoshi emailed Back before publishing the paper. Back went silent on cryptography mailing lists during the exact years Satoshi was active, then resurfaced six weeks after Satoshi vanished.
Key Evidence
Stylometric analysis of 325 nonstandard hyphenation patterns matched Back's writing. A decade before Bitcoin, Back outlined distributed nodes, Hashcash-based coin minting, inflation adjustment, and immutable timestamping. Carreyrou suspects Back's 2008 emails from "Satoshi" were written to himself as cover.

Hal Finney

Cryptographer & First Bitcoin Recipient
Nationality
American
Lived
1956 – 2014
Known For
Reusable Proof-of-Work, PGP 2.0
Accused By
Multiple researchers & journalists
Response
Denied before death
Received the very first Bitcoin transaction from Satoshi. Developed Reusable Proof-of-Work, a concept that inspired Bitcoin's consensus mechanism. Helped test early versions of Bitcoin's software and was among the first to run a node.
Key Evidence
Finney lived blocks away from a man named Dorian Satoshi Nakamoto. His deep cryptographic expertise and early involvement are circumstantial but strong. Against the theory: he was photographed running a 10-mile race in April 2009 while Satoshi was simultaneously sending emails and Bitcoin. He died of ALS in 2014; Satoshi posted a message in 2014.

Nick Szabo

Computer Scientist & Creator of Bit Gold
Nationality
American-Hungarian
Known For
Bit Gold (1998), smart contracts concept
Accused By
Aston University linguistic study (2014), multiple researchers
Response
Denies claim
Created Bit Gold in 1998, widely considered the most direct precursor to Bitcoin. Also coined the term "smart contracts." Szabo's intellectual fingerprints are all over Bitcoin's design philosophy.
Key Evidence
In 2014, Aston University's Centre for Forensic Linguistics analyzed 13 candidates' writing styles and found Szabo's was the closest match to the Bitcoin white paper. Szabo backdated a blog post about Bit Gold to appear written after Bitcoin launched, raising suspicion. Notably, Satoshi never cited Bit Gold in the white paper despite its obvious relevance.

Wei Dai

Cryptographer & Creator of b-money
Nationality
American (of Chinese descent)
Known For
b-money proposal (1998), Crypto++ library
Accused By
Various researchers; cited by Szabo
Response
Has not publicly addressed claims
Created b-money in 1998, a proposal for an anonymous distributed electronic cash system that is directly cited in the Bitcoin white paper. Also created Crypto++, a widely used C++ cryptographic library.
Key Evidence
b-money is one of only two direct predecessors cited by Satoshi. Nick Szabo wrote in 2011 that he, Dai, and Finney were "the only people I know of who liked the idea enough to pursue it to any significant extent." The unit "wei" in Ethereum was named after him, reflecting his foundational influence.

1949
Known For
Sharing the name "Satoshi Nakamoto"

His birth name is Satoshi Nakamoto. A 2014 Newsweek cover story identified him as Bitcoin's creator based on a misinterpreted quote. The story sparked a media frenzy and a car chase by journalists through Temple City, California.
Key Evidence
Beyond sharing the name, Goodman cited his physics background and libertarian leanings. Satoshi himself broke years of silence to post "I am not Dorian Nakamoto" on the P2P Foundation.

Craig Wright

Australian Computer Scientist
Nationality
Australian
Born
1970
Known For
Self-proclaimed Satoshi for 8 years
Accused By
Himself (2016)
Response
Claimed to be Satoshi; ruled fraudulent by UK High Court
The only person to publicly and persistently claim to be Satoshi Nakamoto. After an eight-year campaign of lawsuits, threats, and disputed cryptographic proofs, a UK High Court judge ruled definitively in 2024 that Wright is not Satoshi.
Key Evidence (Against)
Justice Mellor's 231-page ruling found Wright engaged in "industrial scale" forgeries. PGP keys he claimed were from 2008 used ciphers not available until 2009. He was found in contempt of court in December 2024 and given a suspended prison sentence. COPA called his claims "a most serious abuse of the Court's process."
